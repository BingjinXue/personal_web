How State Minimum Wage & EITC Shape a Family's Income, 2015

Each line traces one state's annual income for a married couple with 2 children working at the minimum wage — earnings plus the EITC — as yearly hours rise. The heavy black line is the federal baseline ($7.25/hr, no state credit); the 51 thinner lines are the 50 states + DC. How far the lines spread apart shows how much a family's income depends on which state they live in.

What's plotted. Each line is the annual budget constraint y(h) = w·h + EITC(w·h) for a married couple with 2 children, where w is the state minimum wage and EITC uses the federal 2-child MFJ parameters for the selected tax year. The x-axis is annual hours worked by the household; the y-axis is earnings (= hours × state MW) plus the federal EITC on those earnings plus the state EITC top-up (state_rate × federal_EITC, when on). For example, a point at (2,000 h, $34k) means: working 2,000 hours/year at that state's minimum wage gives earnings of 2000·w, and after adding the federal and state credits the household has $34k of resources.

Pre-tax; nominal dollars. No federal or state income tax is subtracted, and no FICA either — the y-axis is gross earnings plus credits, not take-home pay. See the “Tax treatment” tab for why this is the right framing for showing cross-state policy variation. All dollar amounts are nominal (current dollars of each tax year, not inflation-adjusted), so levels are not directly comparable across different years in real terms.

Where the lines bend. Each line kinks at the EITC thresholds: the credit phases in (steepest segment), plateaus at its maximum, then phases out. Steeper state minimum wages reach those kinks in fewer hours (shifting them left); more generous state EITCs lift the plateau (shifting them up).

The two policy dimensions. Higher state MW rotates the line counter-clockwise: the slope in every segment is steeper, and the kink at the phase-in ceiling, plateau exit, and phase-out exit all shift left in hours-space (since you reach the same earnings level in fewer hours). Higher state EITC raises the plateau and steepens the phase-in slope. The lateral spread of the kinks reflects the cross-section of state MWs; the vertical spread of the plateaus reflects the cross-section of state EITCs.

Why the diagonal pattern. States in the top-left of the envelope have both high MW and a generous state EITC (think CA-2019, DC, MN, NY, VT). States in the bottom-right are at the federal MW with no state top-up (most southern states, MS, AL, TX, etc.) and are drawn behind the heavy federal line.

Reading the variation envelope. The translucent rectangles per category per kink show the (hours, income) range covered by states in that category. Their shadows extend to the x-axis (showing the range of kink-hours) and to the y-axis (showing the range of kink-incomes). When a dimension has no variation, the shadow collapses to a line: MW only has no income variation at the kink (income is fixed at the federal kink height), so its y-shadow is a line; EITC only has no hours variation (hours are fixed at the federal kink-hour), so its x-shadow is a line. The interaction category varies in both dimensions and gets a proper rectangle.

Connecting to labor supply. The slope of each segment is the relevant marginal incentive: in phase-in it's w·(1 + pi_in × s_eff), where s_eff is the effective state EITC rate (the statutory rate if refundable, ~0 if non-refundable for MW workers who owe little state tax); in the plateau it's w; in phase-out it's w·(1 − pi_out × s_eff). Higher state MW rotates every segment counter-clockwise; higher state EITC steepens the phase-in and shallows the phase-out. The cross-state variation in these slopes — together with the variation in kink locations — is what the project's identification strategy targets.
What is and isn't subtracted. The y-axis is gross earnings + federal EITC + state EITC top-up. Federal income tax, state income tax, and FICA (the 7.65% payroll tax) are NOT subtracted. The schedule shown is the pre-tax labor-supply incentive — earnings plus credits — not after-tax disposable income.

Why this framing. The visualization's purpose is to expose cross-state variation in MW and EITC policy. Federal income tax and FICA apply uniformly across all states (no cross-state variation), so subtracting them would shift every line down by the same amount without changing the shape of the envelope or the relative position of state lines. State income tax does vary, but for a married couple with 2 children earning near the federal MW, federal liability is typically zero after the standard deduction, dependent care credit, and CTC offsets, and state liability is similarly small or zero in most states.

Practical implications for the figure.
· The levels shown on the y-axis are slightly higher than what households actually take home — about 7.65% higher across the board from FICA alone.
· The cross-state ranking of lines and the shape of the envelope are unchanged by including taxes, since the omitted components don't vary by state for this household type.
· For net-of-tax labor supply work, one would want to subtract federal income tax, FICA, and state income tax. The other visualization in this project (single-household optimization with α slider) does subtract federal income tax and FICA; that one is the right tool for studying optimal hours. This figure is the right tool for showing policy variation.

What about the Child Tax Credit? Not included. CTC is federal and uniform across states (no cross-state variation), and is partially refundable. If included, it would lift every line by up to $4,000/child (2024) but wouldn't change the cross-state pattern.

What about state EITC interaction with state tax? For non-refundable state EITCs, the credit is capped at the household's state income tax liability. For MW-level workers with 2 children, state tax liability is usually near zero, which is why the Non-refundable handling toggle defaults to “As cash” (statutory rate) but offers “Zero out” (effective rate at low income). See the Refundability tab.
Federal EITC parameters: Tax Policy Center, Earned Income Tax Credit Parameters, 1975–2026 (2-children tier, MFJ phase-out range). MFJ phase-out beginning equals the single phase-out beginning before EGTRRA 2001; the $1k MFJ premium phases in 2002–2004, becomes $2k in 2005–2007 (EGTRRA permanent), $3k in 2008, $5k in 2009–2010 (ARRA), and is made permanent and indexed from 2013 onward (ATRA).

State minimum wages: For 1991–2022, the bundled mw_state_annual.dta is the annual-frequency export from Vaghul & Zipperer (2022), Historical State and Sub-state Minimum Wages, v1.4.0 (Washington Center for Equitable Growth), originally documented in Vaghul & Zipperer (2016). Each state's value is the annual mean of monthly state-effective MWs, so mid-year federal step-ups in 2007/08/09 are reflected. For 2023, UKCPR Jan-1 snapshot. For 2024–2025, US DOL Consolidated Minimum Wage Table cross-checked against the EPI Minimum Wage Tracker, Jan-1 values. State MW = max(state-statutory, federal). The federal reference uses the federal annual mean for years with mid-year changes; $7.25 throughout 2010–2025.

State EITCs (1991–2023): UKY / UKCPR National Welfare Data (July 2025 update), “State EITC Rate” column. For Wisconsin, the 2-dependent rate (11% throughout the sample) is used in place of the headline rate (4%, applies to 1-child households). The “Refundable State EITC” indicator is preserved and the toggle uses it.

State EITCs (2024–2025): ITEP, State Earned Income Tax Credits Support Families and Workers in 2024/2025 — specifically the 2025 Appendix “State Earned Income Tax Credits in 2025” with adjustments for known 2024≠2025 differences (DC 70%→85%, MT 10%→20%, VA 15% partial→20% refundable). California, Minnesota, and Washington use their own credit calculations rather than a simple percentage match; for visualization consistency with the UKCPR encoding (which treats state EITC as state_rate × federal EITC), CA is shown at a representative 45% match, MN at 33%, and WA's flat-amount WFTC is encoded as 0 (since it doesn't multiply federal EITC). These approximations affect those three states specifically; the rest match ITEP's appendix exactly.

Coverage: 1991–2025, all 50 states + DC. Year intersection of all sources.
A state EITC is refundable if households receive the full credit as cash even when it exceeds their state income tax liability. About two-thirds of state EITCs are refundable in any given recent year; the non-refundable ones are limited to offsetting state income tax.

For a married couple with 2 children earning at or near the federal MW, state income tax liability is typically zero or very small (the household sits below state-level filing thresholds or has its liability fully offset by state child-related deductions). In that case a non-refundable state EITC provides “effectively zero” cash to the worker on the relevant range of the budget constraint. The “Zero out” setting reflects this: state lines for non-refundable states collapse onto the “MW only” envelope.

In contrast, “Treat as cash” applies the statutory rate uniformly. This overstates the actual transfer for non-refundable states but is the right object if the question is “what is the statutory marginal incentive” — e.g., for awareness or composition checks.

For empirical work on labor supply this distinction matters: refundable state EITCs are what the Bastian-Jones (2021) and Schanzenbach-Strain (2021) literatures effectively identify off of; non-refundable rates are mostly noise on the low-income margin.
